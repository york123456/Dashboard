The Unusual Return of The Mac

The Pentagon news lit up the entire drone sector. When reports emerged that the U.S. government was exploring direct equity investments in domestic drone manufacturers, Unusual Machines $UMAC, the tiny U.S. drone-component maker, led the group higher and ripped roughly 60% in a single session.

 

From the outside, that move looked like a news-driven lottery ticket. The kind of one-day spike traders read about the next morning and wish they'd caught.

 

It wasn't luck, because the trade had already been positioned a week earlier and the setup had been building for months.

 

This is the anatomy of how it happened. 

 

The Pentagon news was the spark, but the fuse had been there all along.

 

The Setup the Market Wasn't Pricing

 

For most of the past year, Unusual Machines had two stories building beneath the surface that almost nobody outside the small-cap drone space was paying attention to.

 

The political tailwind came first. 

 

In late 2024, Donald Trump Jr. joined the company's advisory board as a shareholder, and from that point forward the company's narrative tied tightly to a single idea: reshoring U.S. drone manufacturing and reducing dependence on Chinese components. 

 

That sounds like a niche story until you remember that drones are now central to modern defense.

 

The fundamental tailwind landed in the Trump administration's proposed FY2027 defense budget, which significantly increased support for next-generation aerospace and defense technologies.

 

Inside that budget was a reported $1 billion "Drone Dominance" initiative aimed at strengthening domestic drone capabilities. 

 

A budget proposal isn't a contract, but for a small company built around the exact theme the budget was funding, it created the conditions for a catalyst-driven move.

 

That was the fundamental case. The chart was telling the same story in a different language.

 

What the Chart Was Saying

Pull up a daily chart of Unusual Machines from earlier this spring and the picture is clean. 

 

The stock had spent months carving out a multi-month base, drifting sideways in a tight range while the broader market chopped around. Volatility was compressing. Each rally got sold a little less aggressively. Each pullback held a little higher.

 

That pattern has a name. 

 

It's called a base, and it matters because bases are where bored money sells to patient money. When a stock stops swinging and starts tightening, sellers have run out of supply and the next move tends to be the kind that catches the market off guard.

 

A compressing base under a defined resistance level, sitting on top of a fundamental story the market hadn't priced, is a coiled spring. 

 

The only question is what releases it.

 

How the Position Was Built

 

Steve Strazza, the chief market strategist at All Star Charts was positioned via call options across multiple expirations during the tightening phase, targeting both June and July strikes while the stock was still consolidating beneath resistance and implied volatility was still cheap.

 

The staggered expirations matter. 

 

Buying only short-dated calls would have been a bet that the catalyst arrived within days. 

 

Buying only long-dated calls would have left money on the table if the move came fast. 

Spreading across two expirations gave the position room to work on either timeline, which is how experienced traders manage the timing uncertainty inside a setup that's clearly coiled but doesn't have a known release date.

 

The First Move, and Why It Mattered

 

The trade started working almost immediately. Within 24 hours of the initial entry, Unusual Machines had its first pop, and Steve sold half the position for a double.

 

That sale is the part most retail traders skip. Taking profits on half the position at a 100% return on the calls pulled the original capital plus the original risk back off the table. 

 

Whatever happened next, the trade could no longer lose money. The remaining half was now a "free trade," running on house money, and the only decision left was how long to let it run.

 

Then the Catalyst Hit

 

Days later, reports surfaced that the Pentagon was exploring direct equity investments in domestic drone manufacturers. The drone sector exploded higher. 

 

Unusual Machines led the group up roughly 60% in a single session.

 

By the time the move was over, the June 18 $20 calls had gone up roughly 17 times their entry price, and the July 17 $20 calls had gone up roughly 9 times. 

 

Steve scaled out aggressively into the spike and redeployed the gains into the next setups already lining up on the watchlist.

 

The headline number is dramatic, but the process behind it was not. 

 

A multi-month base identified early, a fundamental story confirmed underneath, defined-risk calls positioned across multiple expirations while volatility was still cheap, half the position taken off on the first pop to make the rest a free trade, and the catalyst left to do the rest of the work.
