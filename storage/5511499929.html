SPOTLIGHT

Stagnation, inflation and a new supply shock?

The global economic outlook has darkened, according to the Forum’s latest Chief Economists’ Outlook. The conflict in the Middle East and effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz have snapped supply chains and threatened access to energy, food and other essential goods.

Here’s what the report tells us:

📉 Growth expectations have weakened: Of chief economists surveyed, 89% expect global economic growth to soften over the next 12 months, with 21% anticipating a significantly weaker outlook ahead. 

📈 Inflation is heating up again: 94% of respondents expect global inflation to rise over the next year, with elevated crude oil prices identified as the main driver.

⚡Energy and supply chains are taking the biggest hit: Energy, materials and transport services face the highest levels of disruption, while a 30% reduction in traded fertilizer supplies threatens widespread food shortages later this year. 

🌍 Regional outlooks are diverging: The US, India and South-East Asia are seen maintaining stronger trajectories, while Europe faces stagflation risks and the MENA region navigates a sharply deteriorated outlook. 

🤖 AI optimism is cooling: While more than 90% of chief economists expect AI adoption to increase, they also expect productivity gains may take longer to materialize. Benefits are likely to be uneven and depend on investment in skills, infrastructure and new ways of working. 

These themes were also explored in the latest Chief Economists’ Radio Davos episode, where Maersk's Ilaria Maselli discussed the global supply crunch and what it could mean for the months ahead.

IMPACT

Unlocking Africa’s frontier markets

While Africa attracts a disproportionately small share of global capital, it offers highly competitive investment returns and untapped consumer markets. The Humanitarian and Resilience Investing Roadmap for Africa – a new landmark strategy developed by the World Economic Forum and the African Development Bank launched this week – outlines a critical shift from aid dependency to investment-led growth. 

The Roadmap proposes a framework to foster partnerships between civil society, humanitarian and development organizations, governments and private sector companies to mobilize investment in Africa’s frontier markets.

It’s part of the Humanitarian and Resilience Investing (HRI) Initiative, which seeks to bridge humanitarian relief with development finance and private capital amid declining global aid. The initiative aims to mobilize $10 billion to scale 1,000 frontier market businesses by 2030.

Here is how the roadmap is restructuring frontier finance:

🤝 Establishing country alliances: To eliminate domestic business barriers, the roadmap sets up dedicated partnerships led by "country champions" and backed by national governments. These groups will identify investment bottlenecks and map out local business pipelines.

📈 Expanding with a structured plan: Moving away from short-term fixes, the initiative rolls out in three clear phases: alignment, planning, and implementation. The goal is to launch up to five country alliances by 2027 and expand to 10 markets by 2030.

🛡️ Reducing risk for private capital: Fragile markets often present high perceived risks and concerns over political instability. The framework utilizes tools such as guarantees and localized risk assessments to protect private investments and encourage commercial funding to flow into these areas.

🌍 Sourcing locally to boost economies: Instead of purchasing relief supplies globally, aid organizations are encouraged to buy from local markets. This creates reliable revenue streams for local businesses and helps communities shift from aid reliance to sustainable growth.
