TARGETING THE OPPOSITION: TÜRKIYE’S DEMOCRACY AT A CROSSROADS

The MED This Week newsletter provides informed insights on the most significant developments in the MENA region, bringing together unique opinions and reliable foresight on future scenarios. Today, we shed light on the implications of Türkiye’s CHP crisis for Turkish politics and democracy.

Latest developments in Turkish politics cast new shadows on the state of democracy and its implications for the future of a key regional actor. Within a week, an unprecedented court ruling has proclaimed the “absolute nullity” of the 2023 leadership elections of the Republican People’s Party (CHP) – Türkiye’s main opposition party –, effectively replacing Özgür Özel with Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu as the party’s leader. The resulting protests from Özel’s faction have been severely repressed, with the Turkish Police Forces storming the CHP headquarters. Meanwhile, Bilgi University – a private college widely deemed liberal – has been shut down through a presidential decree and reopened three days after widespread mobilisation. Such moves have been regarded as a sign of further setback of democracy in Türkiye. Since the CHP’s sweeping victory at the 2024 local elections under Özel’s leadership, the party has come under increasing pressure from the judiciary, leading to the arrest or replacement of dozens of successful representatives, including Ekrem İmamoğlu, Istanbul’s mayor and CHP’s presidential candidate for the 2028 elections, now in pretrial detention. Kılıçdaroğlu’s contested return to CHP leadership – widely blamed for the party’s string of electoral failures before 2023 – risks deepening internal fragmentation and polarisation, further undermining the party’s prospects ahead of the 2028 general elections. As the electoral campaign comes closer, the event intersects with Erdoğan’s broader strategy to hold on to the presidency as he hits the two-mandates limit. One possible way could entail endorsing a trusted candidate, although no political figure seems popular enough. Alternatively, early elections might allow Erdoğan to compete for a third term. Therefore, prospects for increased fragmentation within the CHP and its controversial leadership might be a step towards the ruling party securing another mandate. Nonetheless, uncertainty over the continued rule of the Justice and Development Party (AKP) and the market volatility following the current judicial controversy highlight that enforcing the grip on state institutions does not necessarily translate into political stability and economic development. As Turkish democracy faces one of the toughest challenges yet, the outcome will be key in determining Ankara’s capacity to avoid instability and a further drift towards authoritarianism.

Experts from the ISPI network discuss how the “absolute nullity” ruling impacts Turkish politics, the economy, and the future of democratic institutions.

 

Political instability casts shadows on Turkish economy

“The recent market turbulence highlights the fragile balance between politics, investor confidence, and economic stability in Türkiye. The court ruling against the chairman of the CHP has intensified concerns over democratic institutions and judicial independence in the country, triggering a sharp reaction in financial markets. The episode demonstrates how political developments can rapidly erode investor confidence in a country already grappling with high inflation, currency volatility, and the disruptive effects of the conflict in Iran and the tensions surrounding the Strait of Hormuz. Beyond the immediate market reaction, the episode raises broader concerns about Türkiye’s political and economic trajectory. In recent years, investors had cautiously returned to Turkish assets after the government adopted more orthodox monetary policies and pledged greater macroeconomic discipline. However, renewed political uncertainty now risks undermining those efforts. Turkish equities fell sharply and fears of capital outflows resurfaced. If political tensions persist, Ankara could face mounting pressure on foreign-exchange reserves, rising borrowing costs, and declining foreign investment at a time when the economy remains highly vulnerable to inflationary pressures and external financing needs.”

Valeria Talbot, ISPI MENA Centre

 

Amid regime transition, the opposition needs to mobilise

“The ousting of Özgür Özel marks the endpoint of an autocratic regime transition underway since Erdoğan’s bloc lost the March 2024 local elections – a process running through the jailing of party’s presidential candidate Ekrem İmamoğlu and some twenty CHP mayors, now seeking to neutralize the main opposition by instrumentalizing the judiciary. That the ruling bloc must reinstate a co-opted former leadership, on legally contestable grounds, betrays its own weakness: it cannot risk the ballot box even on unfair terms. This badly damages the opposition’s institutional capacity, and further steps before any election are likely. The opposition’s remaining strength is popular: a majority rejects further autocratisation. Whether that majority finds a channel of expression will hinge on the opposition’s capacity for novel, creative mobilising strategies. Türkiye’s eighty-year multiparty tradition makes a Russia-style closed autocracy hard to impose – but whether that legacy holds is now an open question.”

Edgar Şar, Co-Director, IstanPol; Research Affiliate, CEU Democracy Institute

 

From party competition to elite accommodation: Türkiye’s shifting political landscape

“The court’s decision to remove Özgür Özel is the latest step in an ongoing campaign to undermine the CHP. Since the arrest of Ekrem İmamoğlu, the party has been under enormous pressure. However, while the decision is bold, it also reveals a more profound transformation of the political landscape. Turkish politics is evolving from institutionalised competition between parties representing societal interests, to an arrangement among elites that increasingly replaces this competition, placing personal interests at the centre. For Kılıçdaroğlu, gaining control of the party and its resources is the primary goal. Meanwhile, securing an opposition that operates within the framework set by Ankara motivates President Erdoğan and his alliance. An elite pact is unfolding, driven by these respective desires for domination, and at the expense of voters, who pose the greatest challenge to achieving these goals.”

Sinem Adar, Head, Centre for Applied Türkiye Studies (CATS) at the Stiftung Wissenschaft und Politik (SWP)

 

The ‘absolute nullity’ ruling: the culmination of Erdoğan’s longstanding judicial capture

“The Turkish court’s decision to remove the CHP leadership is the latest and perhaps most blatant sign of how the country’s judiciary has been transformed into a tool used by President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan against his political opponents. It is the culmination of a long-running process through which Türkiye’s courts have moved from a historically politicised but relatively pluralistic institution into one increasingly subordinated to executive power. The 2010 constitutional referendum, which restructured the Constitutional Court and the High Council of Judges and Prosecutors (HSYK), was presented as a democratising effort aimed at dismantling the old secularist tutelary order. In practice, however, the reforms opened the judiciary to new forms of political capture, first by networks affiliated with the Gülen movement and later by the ruling AKP itself. Following the collapse of the AKP-Gülen alliance after the 2013 corruption investigations, the government moved aggressively to reassert control over the courts and prosecution service. The failed coup attempt in 2016 marked a decisive turning point. Thousands of judges and prosecutors were purged, detained, or dismissed and replaced with figures seen as loyal to the government, creating a widespread climate of fear within the judiciary. The transition to the presidential system in 2017-2018 further institutionalised executive influence over judicial appointments and disciplinary mechanisms, which weakened the separation of powers and transformed the judiciary into a central instrument of political control.”

Gönül Tol, Senior Fellow, Middle East Institute
