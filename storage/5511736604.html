Greetings,

I’m Courtney Kennedy, vice president of methods and data innovation at Pew Research Center. Since you’re a new subscriber, I’d like to offer you a brief tour of the foundation that underlies all of our work: our methods. 

Where does our data come from?

Here at the Center, we conduct public opinion polling, demographic research, content analysis and other data-driven social science research. A primary source for our U.S. data is the American Trends Panel (ATP), a nationally representative, online survey panel of more than 12,000 randomly selected U.S. adults who have agreed to take repeated surveys over time. We recruit panelists by mail using the Postal Service’s master list of residential addresses. This helps ensure our sample is representative of the U.S. adult population. The panelists take their surveys online or by telephone with a live interviewer. 

Our global survey findings are drawn from our cross-national surveys, which are carefully crafted to ensure the comparability of data across multiple languages, cultures and contexts. We also mine a wide variety of publicly available data sources to conduct our demographic research.

All of our published research is accompanied by a “How we did this” box, which describes how and why we conducted our analysis. We believe strongly in the value of transparency and in helping the public understand polling, and how to be better poll consumers. Wondering how we ensure the findings in our publications are accurate? Read this post for an explanation of how we check our numbers and facts.

What does a methodologist do?

Our team of methodologists partners with researchers across the Center to support and advise them throughout the survey research process, from crafting questionnaires to analyzing survey findings to “cleaning” data for release to the public. We ensure the Center’s data is rigorously collected and accurately represents the opinions of the populations we set out to survey.

Our international survey methods team oversees our survey work in countries around the world, partnering with reputable local research organizations and survey field houses to design, manage and ensure overall quality for our cross-national surveys.

Our team also conducts experiments to support and continually improve the Center’s data collection and analysis. We explore questions facing the world of public opinion research, from the rise of online polling to the impact of low response rates and more.

Exploring new methodological frontiers

In addition to expanding and strengthening the traditional methodologies that underlie survey research, the Center also explores the potential of new methods of measuring public opinion.

For example, our Data Labs team uses computational methods to complement and expand on the Center’s existing research agenda. The team collects text, network and behavioral datasets; uses innovative computational techniques and empirical strategies for analysis; and generates original research. Data Labs also explores the limitations of these methods and works toward establishing standards for use and analysis.
