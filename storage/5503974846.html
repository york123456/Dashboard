Georgia at a Geopolitical Crossroads: Iranian Influence and Strategic Competition

Georgia was once one of the United States’ most dependable strategic partners in the Black Sea region. Today, however, its government, led by the Georgian Dream party, has grown more authoritarian while aligning more closely with US adversaries, particularly the Islamic Republic of Iran. On Thursday, Matt Boyse, Luke Coffey, Giorgi Kandelaki, Ian McGinnity, and Representative Joe Wilson (R-SC) examined Tehran’s expanding influence in Georgia. Hudson President and CEO John P. Walters delivered introductory remarks.

Watch the event, listen to the podcast, or read the transcript here. 

 
 

Key Insights

1. Evidence shows that the Georgian Dream–led government increasingly supports Iran.

“We should look honestly at Georgian Dream as it supports the terrorist regime of Tehran. In February 2026, the Georgian Dream [deputy] foreign minister attended an Iranian embassy event celebrating the anniversary of the 1979 Iranian Revolution. . . . Additionally, Tbilisi’s TV Tower was illuminated in Iranian flag colors. . . . A Georgian Dream government representative grotesquely and revealingly signed a condolence book at the Iranian embassy, mourning the dictator Khamenei . . . in the very year that dictator Khamenei had killed 50,000 Iranian citizens. . . . Georgian Dream has helped Iran in the current war by allowing strategic Russian airlifters to transit its airspace on route to Iran, carrying supplies to help the Iranian regime.”

— Representative Joe Wilson (R-SC)

 

2. While many people understand that Georgia has moved toward Russia, Tbilisi’s support for Iran is less well-known. 

“Many people have thought that Georgia’s slide away from the West, and the United States in particular, is just about Russia. And the message of this report—one of the messages that I and Luke hoped to put forth—is that that is not the case. This trajectory away from the civilized world has this Iranian dimension. . . . For the last few years, the Islamic Republic of Iran has been able to find a comfortable environment in which it successfully built formidable influence infrastructure, which consists of whole ecosystem of organizations that, in this city or in other Western capitals, are regarded as [Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps] fronts that the IRGC uses for recruitment of individuals in various countries and then subsequently for operations, against the United States in particular.”

— Giorgi Kandelaki

3. Georgian Dream needs to crack down on the Iranian threat.

“So, what can the Georgian Dream do? What should the United States expect this government to do? Firstly, [Tbilisi] needs to show that it wants to be America’s partner. I think the ball is very much in the Georgian Dream’s court right now. They have this idea that they are the populists, the MAGA of Georgia, and nothing could be further from the truth. . . . So, the first thing they need to do is demonstrate . . . that they’re serious about cracking down on this Iranian threat. You don’t have to go to the Iranian embassy to sign a condolence book, okay? . . . But then I think we need to have a more visible crackdown on some of these networks that exist that are outlined in the report that Giorgi just mentioned . . . because this is not in America’s interest, and it’s not in Georgia’s interest as well.”

— Luke Coffey
