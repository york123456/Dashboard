Autonomy at scale

In the news. Autonomous robots are moving beyond pilots and into real-world operations. The World Economic Forum notes that robots can now work continuously in industrial spaces such as ports and warehouses, including moving shipping containers without human intervention. Still, experts say that full autonomy is years away: Costs remain high, standards vary, and robots perform best in tightly controlled settings. The next frontier for robotic intelligence is adapting to unpredictable environments and manipulating objects safely, which one executive says will require better hardware and improved risk assessment. [WEF]

Understanding where supply chain constraints cluster and where the supplier base is already equipped for scale is essential for manufacturers managing sourcing risk, suppliers prioritizing investment, and end users assessing deployment timelines.

On McKinsey.com. Humanoid robotics could represent the most significant opportunity for suppliers in a generation. By removing constraints from humanoid robots’ supply chain—an area that’s attracting rapidly growing funding—companies could scale up the technology and realize a multibillion-dollar opportunity. According to McKinsey’s Ani Kelkar, Christian Jansen, Erik Sparre, Mark Patel, Mikael Robertson, and coauthors, demand for humanoids could continue to rise sharply in the coming years. Suppliers can prepare now by partnering to develop humanoid-specific components, investing in safety and certification, and building revenue streams across a humanoid’s full life cycle.﻿

Capturing AI’s full advantage﻿

In the news. As companies race to deploy AI agents built on the same underlying models, many risk erasing the very differentiation that creates competitive advantage, according to Harvard Business Review. Some experts call this the “agentic convergence trap”: When organizations rely on similar AI systems, their decisions, workflows, and customer experiences can become similar or even identical. Companies can avoid that outcome by establishing strong governance (namely, by keeping humans involved in key decisions), then building systems around proprietary data and company-specific objectives, and developing ways to measure whether their AI systems are converging with those of competitors. [HBR]

On McKinsey.com. With AI adoption exploding, the current challenge for leaders is not whether to deploy it but how to build advantages that their competitors can’t replicate. McKinsey’s Dago Diedrich, Evan Williams, Tanguy Catlin, and Tim Fountaine explain several ways that companies can build “competitive moats,” including economies of scale, embeddedness in core work, and high-velocity learning and deployment. Of the nine moats the authors describe, leaders can start by focusing on the one to three areas where their companies already have advantages, then committing investments and making hard trade-offs to strengthen them.

Breaking up to grow﻿

In the news. Corporate spin-offs are gaining momentum in the market, Bloomberg reports. In the first quarter of 2026, one benchmark showed that US spin-off stocks outperformed the S&P 500 by their widest margin in six years. This trend reflects a shift away from the long-held view that scale and diversification necessarily produce greater shareholder value. Now, investors seem to prefer simpler, more-focused businesses with clearer strategies and operations, especially if the spin-off is a well-known asset in its own right. But breakups also carry risks, including debt burdens, greater exposure to volatility, and potential exclusion from major indexes that attract institutional investors. [Bloomberg]

On McKinsey.com. Companies considering a spin-off may be overlooking one of the biggest determinants of success: restructuring the asset before the separation takes place. In their research, McKinsey’s Anna Mattsson, Anthony Luu, and Kevin Van Ingelgem found that when parent companies improve a spin-off’s readiness to operate independently—by redefining decision rights, resetting the cost base, or upgrading governance—the spin-off tends to see better returns and margins. Leaders can improve outcomes by activating the future entity’s strategy early, carefully evaluating exit options and buyer requirements, and rigorously benchmarking cost structures before the transaction.
