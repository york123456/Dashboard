Shooting Down Civil Aircraft: What International and U.S. Law Say About a Charge in the Raul Castro Indictment

Last week, the U.S. Department of Justice unsealed an indictment of the former Cuban president Raul Castro on charges that include the destruction of a civil aircraft in violation of 18 U.S.C. section 32. The incident underlying that charge involved the 1996 shootdown by Cuban fighter jets of two civil aircraft used by the Cuban exiles known as the “Brothers to the Rescue” to engage in pro-democracy activities and to rescue individuals attempting to flee Cuba by sea. According to the indictment, the shootdown caused the deaths of four individuals, including three U.S. citizens. Fidel Castro, Raul’s brother and predecessor, stated at the time that he had given orders instructing the shootdown of aircraft violating Cuban airspace (according to the DOJ indictment, it was Raul in his then-role as head of the military who authorized deadly action against the Brothers’ aircraft, specifically). It is contested whether the civil aircraft were in Cuban or international airspace at the time of the shootdown. Cuban officials claimed that the fighter jets issued warnings before engaging in lethal use of force, although the United States took the position at the United Nations Security Council that Cuba had failed to follow proper warning procedures, and that in any case the use of weapons against a civil aircraft in flight was a violation of international law (see pg. 629-30).

Actions Toward Universities
Bakken v. United States Military Academy (S.D.N.Y.) 
Case No. 7:25-cv-07826

May 26, 2026: Judge Seibel granted Plaintiff’s motion for a preliminary injunction and denied Defendants’ motion to dismiss, finding that the court had subject matter jurisdiction over Plaintiff’s First Amendment challenge and that he was likely to succeed on the merits. The court enjoined Defendants from enforcing the February 2025 Academic Engagement Policy – which required civilian West Point faculty to obtain prior approval before any external writing, speaking, or media engagement within their academic expertise that referenced their West Point affiliation – against all USMA civilian faculty, and separately barred Defendants from prohibiting Plaintiff from expressing his opinions to students on the subjects he teaches. The court found that Defendants’ stated justifications appeared to be “post-hoc rationalizations” and that the policy was likely “a pretext for an effort to channel speech to conform to the President’s liking and to prevent dissenting views from entering the marketplace of ideas.”

Equal Credit Opportunity Act Regulations
National Fair Housing Alliance v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (D.D.C.) 
Case No. 1:26-cv-01820

On April 22, 2026, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), under Acting Director Russell Vought, issued a final rule amending Regulation B, 12 C.F.R. Part 1002, which implements the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA). The rule makes three significant changes: it eliminates disparate-impact liability under ECOA; narrows the prohibition against discriminatory discouragement of prospective credit applicants; and limits Special Purpose Credit Programs (SPCPs) for for-profit organizations — including by explicitly barring SPCPs based on race, color, national origin, or sex. 

On May 27, 2026, Plaintiffs — two fair lending nonprofits and two fair lending consultancy firms — filed a complaint alleging the new rule reduces demand for their services and thwarts their mission-driven work to expand equitable credit access. Plaintiffs allege the rule is arbitrary and capricious under the APA, 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A), for departing from longstanding regulatory interpretations without reasoned explanation; contrary to law under 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A) because the rule's elimination of disparate-impact liability, narrowing of discouragement protections, and SPCP restrictions conflict with ECOA’s text and purpose; in excess of statutory authority under 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(C) for purporting to create safe harbors from statutory liability; and without observance of required procedure under 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(D) for failing to convene a SBREFA panel, prepare required regulatory flexibility analyses, provide adequate notice-and-comment, and meaningfully respond to significant comments. Plaintiffs also assert that Acting CFPB Director Vought lacks lawful authority to serve as Acting Director since he was neither nominated nor confirmed by the Senate, rendering the rule ultra vires. Plaintiffs seek vacatur of the rule and declaratory and injunctive relief.

 

Disclosure of Special Counsel Hur Investigation Records
Biden v. Department of Justice (D.D.C.) 
Case No. 1:26-cv-01818

Former President Joseph R. Biden, Jr., sued the Department of Justice to block its planned June 15, 2026 release of audio recordings and transcripts of his private 2016–2017 conversations with ghostwriter Mark Zwonitzer, which were recorded at Biden’s home in connection with the writing of his memoir Promise Me, Dad. The Department obtained the materials in 2023 during Special Counsel Robert K. Hur’s investigation into Biden’s handling of classified records, which closed without charges. For approximately two years, the Department defended withholding the materials under FOIA Exemptions 5, 6, and 7(C) in Heritage Foundation v. U.S. Dep’t of Just., No. 24-cv-645 (D.D.C), representing to the court that disclosure would constitute “a severe invasion of privacy with little to no meaningful or cognizable counterbalancing public interest.” In February 2026, the Department reversed its position and notified Biden of its intent to release the materials to the Heritage plaintiffs. On March 23, 2026, after Biden’s counsel raised objections, the Department produced a letter from House Judiciary Committee Chair Jim Jordan requesting the same materials to advance the Committee’s “oversight of the politicization of the Biden-Garland Department of Justice.”

On May 26, 2026, Biden filed a complaint alleging that DOJ’s decision to release the materials is arbitrary and capricious and an abuse of discretion in violation of the APA, 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A), because the Committee's request is pretextual, lacks a legitimate legislative purpose, and falls outside Congress’s Article I investigative powers; and that the proposed disclosure violates the Privacy Act, 5 U.S.C. § 552a, because Biden has not consented and no statutory exception applies. Biden seeks a declaratory judgment that the Committee’s request is invalid and unenforceable, vacatur of DOJ’s disclosure decision, and preliminary and permanent injunctive relief barring disclosure to the Committee.
