NORC Launches Civic Health & Elections Project

NORC at the University of Chicago has launched the Civic Health & Elections Project, a nonpartisan, 50-state study of more than 85,000 registered voters, with data made publicly available immediately after the 2026 election.

The NORC Civic Health & Elections Project is designed to improve civic discourse and decision-making across the United States. It combines a large-scale survey with external data to provide unprecedented state-level depth and rapid, accessible data tools for communities, media, and policymakers to strengthen accountability and civic confidence.

NORC also is offering AmeriSpeak® Voter Surveys, which enable individual researchers and organizations to customize and extend the Civic Health & Elections study. Through this omnibus-style survey series, researchers can field custom questions to 1,000-5,000 registered voters before the election, after it, or both, while following the same respondents over time.

Palisades Fire Survey Reveals Rebuilding Priorities and Barriers to Return

A year after Southern California’s Palisades Fire, only about a quarter of residents had returned, and just 13 percent of heavily impacted homeowners had broken ground on construction, according to a landmark NORC at the University of Chicago study for the Pacific Palisades Community Council (PPCC).

To reach this scattered population, we used U.S. Postal Service delivery-sequence files, voter records, and commercial databases with geographic information systems to verify addresses within the fire zone. We built an unusually comprehensive contact list of about 9,000 households, resulting in a probability-based response from 1,265 verified residents. The data filled gaps and overcame scamming fears by co-branding with the PPCC and reaching residents through its social media channels.

We found an interlocking web of insurance barriers, environmental fears, and stalled infrastructure decisions keeping residents from returning. We will launch a follow-up survey later this year that will offer California’s decision-makers and stakeholders fresh data to add to the first comprehensive, statistically representative picture of a diverse neighborhood still in recovery long after one of the state’s most destructive wildfires.
