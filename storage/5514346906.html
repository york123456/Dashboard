What happens when the public case for new nuclear weapons tells adversaries more than it tells Congress? How did Beijing use America's economic fragility during the Iran war as a live case study in constraining U.S. power? Why are countries whose navies were never designed to patrol vast ocean territories now treating their exclusive economic zones like sovereign land? What does it mean to be a frontline journalist in Ukraine in 2026? And with Khamenei gone and his son holding a title without authority, who is actually running Iran?

War on the Rocks contributors tackled these questions, among many others, this week. Read below.

Contriving Imaginary Gaps in Nuclear Deterrence by Jay Tilden

Glass Jaw? The New Economic Fragility Recasting American Power by Andy Polk

Beijing spent years studying how to constrain U.S. action without matching U.S. military power. The Iran war just gave them a live case study in how economic fragility does the work for them.

 

How America Lost Its Most Important Defense Tech Habit by Jack Barry

American export controls have traditionally depended on an unspoken civic duty. Is that duty eroding at the worst possible moment?

 

Hollow Deals, Tricky Negotiations, and State Visits by WOTR Staff (Members Only)

A Sea Control Revolution? by Richard Dunley 

Countries now treat their exclusive economic zones like sovereign territory. Their navies never signed up to defend that much ocean.

 

What Everyone is Missing About North Korea’s Reunification Strategy by Hyunseung Lee

Control Without Ownership: How China’s Party-Business Networks Dominate Indonesia’s Mineral Supply Chains by Ahmad Syarif

Washington Shouldn’t Fly Solo on Building Space Superiority by Nazmelis Zengin (Members Only)

What Did the NPT Review Conference Achieve? with Ankit Panda, Kingston Reif, Christopher Ford, Kelsey Davenport, and Astrid Chevreuil (Members Only)

Since the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty conference last agreed on anything, nuclear facilities have been bombed, arsenals expanded, and non-nuclear status reconsidered. Sixteen years later, the conference still has no consensus.

 

The Road to Space Runs through the Poles by Rebecca Pincus and David Marsh

Why are there more antennas on a tiny Arctic archipelago than almost anywhere else on Earth?

 

Leading in the Dark: How Submarine Commanders Think Under Uncertainty by Paulo Frade

Understanding the Value of Ukrainian Railways with WOTR Staff (Members Only)

Sustainment runs on rail for both sides in this war. Both sides know it. Neither is letting the other forget it.

 

An Inconvenient Reality: Climate-Preparedness Cuts Are Lethality Cuts by Caitlin Irby 

A single flood at one Air Force base cost $1.2 billion to repair, the same amount being cut from the climate and geological programs that help prevent it.

 

The U.S. Military Lacks an Ethics Doctrine. Combat Effectiveness Suffers by Dennis W. Katolin 

The military spends years hardening bodies and sharpening minds for combat. It spends almost nothing preparing warfighters for the moment their values collide with the reality of war.

 

Rebuilding American Manufacturing: A Keystone for Economic Statecraft by Mark Rosenblatt 

In 2010, Beijing cut rare-earth exports to Japan over a fishing dispute near the Senkaku Islands. Washington watched, learned nothing, and spent the next 15 years deepening its own dependencies.

 

The Lessons of Sacrifice by Adam A. Scher 

Memorial Day is not only about wars filed away in history, but also about lives lost in service to the nation and the obligations those losses place on the living.
