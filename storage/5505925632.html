AI can chart a course to disaster faster than humans can notice

Researchers at King's College London gave three commercially available AI models a tabletop exercise typically used to train military strategists. Each system played the leader of a nuclear-armed country in a Cold War-style standoff. The models chose to use tactical nuclear weapons in all but one of 21 games. As Hiranya Peiris writes, "As more autonomous systems are given consequential tasks with less human oversight, the risks from ungoverned paths multiply." Read more.

African countries can still get US funding for public health—if they cough up minerals and data first

New 'transactional' US global health agreements offer critical US aid in combatting HIV and other health threats in return for what critics say is expansive US access to resources like data and minerals, reports Matt Field. Read more.

Lessons learned from an Army Corps radioactive waste disposal plan for Michigan that went South

With no warning, in 2023 the US Army Corps of Engineers awarded a $40 million contract to relocate radioactive waste from a site in New York to Michigan's Wayne County. Michiganders were able to prevent the move, and the Army Corps "learned a valuable lesson about the importance of transparency and has promised to do better by communities in the future," writes Chanese A. Forté. Read more.

Sorry, climate change is still dangerous, no matter what nonsense Trump emits

"Arms control is not about making agreements with your friends"—Interview with Jane Vaynman

Arms control in a post-Trump administration world, and under conditions of considerable technological change, would be very much a work in progress, says Jane Vaynman. The goal now is to do the thinking—about what arms control should look like, and for what threats—so that when conditions do shift, we're positioned to act on them.
