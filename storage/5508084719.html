Planning for America’s Democratic Renewal Must Start Now: Lessons from Poland

Rebuilding democratic institutions and norms after illiberal rule is difficult, as Poles have learned throughout their post-communist transition to democracy after 1989. In 2023, Polish voters successfully voted out the right-wing Law and Justice (PiS) party after eight years of illiberal rule and democratic backsliding. The advice from Poles — government officials, civil society activists, and analysts — to American democrats (with a lower case “d”) is to start planning now for how to build a framework for democratic renewal.

Delegation of Tariff Authority by Other Means

In February 2026, the Supreme Court struck down the Trump administration’s expansive tariff regime, holding in Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump that the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) does not authorize the president to impose tariffs on imports from every country in the world. Within weeks, the U.S. Trade Representative (USTR) responded by launching two sets of investigations under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, which authorizes investigation into other countries’ practices that may burden or restrict U.S. commerce.

Housing of Transgender Incarcerated Individuals
Kingdom v. Trump (D.D.C.)
Case No. 1:25-cv-00691

May 26, 2026: Judge Lamberth granted Plaintiffs’ motion for a renewed preliminary injunction, extending the injunction blocking Defendants from implementing Section 4(c) of Executive Order 14168 against class members for a period not to exceed 90 days beginning June 1, 2026, and ending fourteen calendar days after the court resolves the pending motion for an updated preliminary injunction. The court also ordered BOP to restore and maintain access to gender-affirming treatment modalities for those who previously received them pursuant to a BOP staff prescription, and found that Plaintiffs and certified class showed a substantial likelihood of success on the merits and that applying the ban would cause immediate, irreparable harm.

 

Anti-Weaponization Fund
Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington v. U.S. Department of Justice (D.D.C.) 
Case No. 1:26-cv-01789

On May 18, 2026, the Department of Justice entered into a settlement agreement with President Donald Trump, his sons, and the Trump Organization in Trump v. IRS, No. 26-cv-20609 (S.D. Fla.), a lawsuit Trump had filed against the IRS and Department of Treasury seeking more than $10 billion for the unauthorized disclosure of his tax returns. The settlement, together with an accompanying order by Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, purports to create an “Anti-Weaponization Fund” capitalized with $1.776 billion drawn from the Treasury’s Judgment Fund and administered by five members appointed by the AG and removable by the President without cause. The settlement and order purport to exempt the Fund is allegedly structured to operate outside the Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), the Federal Records Act (FRA), and the Administrative Procedure Act (APA).

On May 22, 2026, Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington (CREW), a nonprofit government watchdog organization, filed a complaint against DOJ, Blanche, Treasury, Secretary Scott Bessent, the IRS, IRS CEO Frank Bisignano, the Anti-Weaponization Fund, and the Fund’s five unnamed members. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants usurped Congress’s Article I powers (Spending/Appropriations Clauses) by creating and funding a new agency without statutory authorization, and invokes the court’s inherent equitable power to enjoin that conduct; that the May 18 settlement and order are contrary to law and in excess of statutory authority under the APA, 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A) and (C); that Defendants failed to conduct required notice-and-comment rulemaking under the APA, 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(D); that the Fund’s secrecy structure violates the First Amendment by suppressing speech Defendants disfavor; that Defendants acted arbitrarily and capriciously under the APA, 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(A); and that the Fund’s non-compliance with FOIA and FRA is ultra vires. Plaintiffs seek declarations that the Fund is a de facto “agency” subject to FOIA and the FRA and that the May 18 settlement and order are unlawful; vacatur of the settlement and order; injunctions barring Treasury from transferring the $1.776 billion and barring the Fund from disbursing any awards; and a writ of mandamus compelling compliance with FRA and FOIA obligations.
