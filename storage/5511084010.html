Follow the Law, not the Plan: Legal Considerations for Third States in Gaza

On May 21, the United Nations Security Council convened its monthly briefing on the situation in the Middle East, including the Palestinian question – and with it, the first “accountability moment” under Resolution 2803, adopted six months earlier. That Resolution had endorsed the 20-point plan for Gaza presented by US President Donald Trump (“Trump plan”), welcomed the establishment of the “Board of Peace” (BoP) as a transitional governance administration for Gaza, and authorized the deployment of an “International Stabilization Force” (ISF). Shortly before the Resolution was issued, the United States had established a Civil-Military Coordination Centre (CMCC) in Israel to “facilitate the flow of humanitarian, logistical, and security assistance” into Gaza. The CMCC, which operates as a joint Israeli–US structure, without Palestinian representation, has reportedly been involved in a much broader range of issues, spanning reconstruction, civil governance and lawmaking.

The Weaponization of GLOMAG: How Rivals Co-opt U.S. Sanctions to Target Business and Political Opponents

When Congress established new tools to attack global corruption and protect human rights, it intended to hold responsible corrupt officials and human-rights abusers who often operate beyond the reach of the judicial system. These legislative authorities are used by the executive branch to implement restrictive measures ranging from visa bans to full economic and trade sanctions. The Global Magnitsky Human Rights Accountability Act – named after Sergei Magnitsky, the Russian tax lawyer who died in pretrial detention after exposing a massive state fraud – created the most expansive extraterritorial financial sanctions program that the United States had ever assembled. While authority under Section 7031(c) of the State Department’s annual appropriations legislation focuses on barring entry to the United States, it nonetheless causes enough reputational damage to sever banking relationships, collapse business partnerships, and destroy political careers.

Election Law
DSCC v. Trump (D.D.C.) 
Case No. 1:26-cv-01114

League of United Latin American Citizens v. Executive Office of the President (D.D.C.)
1:26-cv-01132

NAACP v. Donald J. Trump (D.D.C.)
1:26-cv-01151

May 28, 2026: Judge Nichols denied Plaintiffs’ motions for a preliminary injunction, holding that Plaintiffs had failed to establish Article III standing or ripeness as to either challenged provision of Executive Order 14399. As to Section 2(a) (directing DHS to compile and transmit State Citizenship Lists to state election officials), the court found any injury too speculative because no lists have been created, no infrastructure established, and the order imposes no obligations on states to act on the lists. As to Section 3(b) (directing USPS to initiate rulemaking on mail-in ballot design and absentee participation lists), the court found the claims not yet ripe because no notice of proposed rulemaking has been issued and any concrete harm depends on multiple contingencies yet to materialize. Judge Nichols noted that the Plaintiffs were also unlikely to succeed on the merits of their ultra vires claim, since the challenged Executive Order operates as internal executive branch guidance and is “subject to the limits of existing laws,” noting that “whether the government’s actions under the Order are lawful or unlawful will depend on how those agencies decide to implement the Order, not on the Order itself.” 

Anti-Weaponization Fund
Gill v. Department of Justice (S.D. Cal.)

On May 18, 2026, the Department of Justice (DOJ) announced the establishment of the “Anti-Weaponization Fund” as part of the settlement of Trump v. IRS, No. 26-cv-20609 (S.D. Fla.), a lawsuit filed in January 2026 by President Donald Trump, his sons, and the Trump Organization against the IRS and Department of Treasury over the unauthorized disclosure of their tax returns. Pursuant to an accompanying order by Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, the settlement creates a five-member commission, appointed by the Attorney General and removable by the President, to receive and adjudicate claims by individuals asserting harm from “Lawfare” or “Weaponization” — defined in the settlement as the improper use of government power for political or ideological purposes — and authorizes $1.776 billion from the Judgment Fund to pay approved claims, with a December 2028 sunset date.

On May 28, 2026, Allison M. Gill, a former Department of Veterans Affairs employee and co-founder of the Mueller, She Wrote podcast who alleges she was targeted by a VA investigation in 2019 because of her public criticism of President Trump, filed a complaint in the Southern District of California. The complaint alleges that the Fund Establishment Order and the DOJ Notice constitute a legislative rule subject to the APA’s notice-and-comment requirements, 5 U.S.C. § 553, and that Defendants violated the APA by issuing those rules without publishing notice in the Federal Register or affording interested parties an opportunity to comment, 5 U.S.C. § 706(2)(D). Plaintiff further alleges that the failure to engage in rulemaking deprived her of the opportunity to comment on the Fund’s definitions of “Lawfare” and “Weaponization,” the basis for the $1.776 billion cap, potential Appointments Clause infirmities in the Fund’s structure, and the constitutionality under the Fourteenth Amendment, Section 4, of paying claims by individuals convicted in connection with the January 6, 2021 attack on the Capitol. Plaintiff seeks a declaration that Defendants’ actions violated the APA, vacatur of the Fund Establishment Order and DOJ Notice, preliminary and permanent injunctive relief barring implementation of the Fund pending APA-compliant rulemaking, and temporary relief to prevent depletion of the Fund’s corpus during litigation.

Denial of Federal Grants
Council for Opportunity in Education v. U.S. Department of Education (D.D.C.)
Case No. 1:25-cv-03491

May 27, 2026: Judge Chutkan granted in part and denied in part Defendants’ motions to dismiss the consolidated cases, dismissing COE’s ultra vires and mandamus claims in both cases and its standalone constitutional claims in the TRIO case, while allowing COE’s constitutional APA claims under the Spending and Take Care Clauses in the SSS case to proceed. The court also denied the remaining portion of COE’s motion to extend the preliminary injunction to 33 unidentified members who had submitted no evidence of irreparable harm.
